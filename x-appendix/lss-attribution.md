# LSS Attribution — How the Ledger Remembers

*(or, Who Gets Credit and How Credit is Earned)*

> **TL;DR:** Lifetime Syntropy Score (LSS) is not awarded only to the Node that fills a Vacuum. The Node that *identified* the Vacuum — the one who spotted the problem, articulated the need, and posted it to the Stigmergic Board — earns 20% of the LSS credit. Seeing the leak is Syntropy. Fixing it is Syntropy. Both are recorded.

---

## The Problem with Pure Executor Attribution

In the fiat world, credit flows to the person who writes the code, lays the bricks, or delivers the presentation. The person who identified the *need* — who noticed that the bridge was cracking, who realized the API was failing, who saw that the community needed a bakery — is invisible. They receive no credit, no royalties, no attribution.

This is a thermodynamic error. 

Identifying a real Vacuum requires intelligence, perception, and energy. A Node who consistently spots high-value problems and articulates them clearly on the Stigmergic Board is generating Syntropy — they are creating the *signal* that organizes the kinetic energy of other Nodes. Without the signal, the energy would remain diffuse and undirected. The scout who finds the water source deserves attribution alongside the engineer who builds the well.

---

## The 80/20 LSS Split

When a Vacuum is posted on the Stigmergic Board and subsequently filled by a Battery (executor), the Lifetime Syntropy Score generated by the circuit is split:

| Recipient | LSS Share | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **The Executor** (Battery — the Node who did the work) | **80%** | They performed the kinetic labor. They transformed potential energy into physical reality. |
| **The Creator** (Vacuum — the Node/Pod/Hub that posted the problem) | **20%** | They generated the signal. They identified a real need, articulated it clearly, and created the gravitational pull that organized the work. |

### Example

A scout (a Node) discovers that a local manufacturing firm is manually processing invoices, creating massive delays. The scout doesn't know how to code, but they identify the Vacuum, negotiate a B2B arrangement (via their Hub membrane), and bring the project to their Pod. The scout defines exactly what the firm needs. A Builder in the Pod claims the Vacuum and develops the automated pipeline over three weeks. The circuit closes at `50c`.

- The **executor** (the Builder) earns `80% × 50 = 40 LSS points` on their Lifetime Syntropy Score.
- The **creator** (the scout who found and structured the deal) earns `20% × 50 = 10 LSS points`.

If the Vacuum was acquired collaboratively by the Pod rather than an individual Node, the 20% LSS is distributed equally among the Pod members, or by Pod consensus.

---

## Why 80/20?

The ratio mirrors the Syntropy Split itself. In the Split, 80% goes to the Node who executed the labor, and 20% goes to the system that organized, sustained, and directed that labor. The LSS Attribution follows the same physics: 80% to the hands that built, 20% to the eyes that saw.

This ratio is a **Constitutional Default** — it cannot be changed by local governance. It is hardcoded into the ledger because attribution games are the single fastest path to corruption. If Pods could set their own LSS splits, they would inevitably be gamed: a leader could claim 50% LSS attribution for every Vacuum they post, inflating their LSS without doing kinetic work. The fixed 80/20 prevents this.

## Self-Executed Vacuums

What happens when a Node posts a Vacuum and then fills it themselves? They receive both the 80% and the 20% — a full 100% of the LSS credit. This is correct, because they performed both functions: they identified the need *and* they solved it. There is no double-counting, because the circuit only fires once. You cannot split a circuit you executed alone into two separate LSS events.

---

## The Observer Interaction

The LSS Attribution interacts with the **Observer Effect** (documented in *Appendix: Engineering Antifragility, Section 8*). A circuit that opens and closes entirely within a sealed Pod — with no external observer — generates **local utility** but does not generate **global LSS**. The 80/20 split applies only to circuits that are witnessed by an out-of-Pod observer (an external client, a peer auditor, or a cross-Pod collaborator). This prevents wash trading: you cannot inflate your LSS by posting fake Vacuums and immediately filling them yourself within a closed Pod.

## LSS Attribution and Sharing the Harvest

The LSS Attribution and Sharing the Harvest (see *Appendix: Sharing the Harvest*) operate on different axes:

*   **LSS Attribution** governs **who gets credit** — the signal. It determines your reputation and governance weight over time. The 80/20 split between Executor and Vacuum-creator is constitutional and cannot be changed.
*   **Sharing the Harvest** governs **who gets bounty** — the energy. The level system (L0–L4) determines your compensation tier based on your role and the Pod's maturity stage.

Both feed into the **Redistribution Weight** — the factor that determines your precise position within your level's compensation range. Your LSS (earned through credited circuits over your lifetime) establishes your seniority score. Combined with Throughput and Join Date, this determines how much of your level's range you receive. A Node who consistently identifies high-value Vacuums accumulates LSS credit through the 20% attribution, which over time elevates their Redistribution Weight even if their kinetic role is Builder-level (L2). The scout and the builder are both building their future weight.
